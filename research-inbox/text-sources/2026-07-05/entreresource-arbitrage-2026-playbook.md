## Content (condensed)

Core thesis: arbitrage isn't hard because the concept is hard — it's hard because "buy low sell high"
leaves out the math, restrictions, and operational grind that decide whether a deal makes money.
Treat it as a system (sourcing → analysis → prep → replenishment), not a hustle.

### Five-point pre-buy filter
Before any buy: (1) can I sell the brand, (2) does the listing have steady demand, (3) how many FBA
sellers are on it, (4) what does the price history look like, (5) what margin is left after every
reasonably expected cost. "A cheap product is only cheap. A real arbitrage buy has demand, room after
fees, and a listing structure you can compete in."

### Strict buy order (protects cash)
1. Scan the product → 2. confirm eligibility → 3. review listing for brand/condition/compliance issues
→ 4. check fees, price history, seller count → 5. decide quantity LAST. Beginners reverse this because
the discount tag creates urgency — that's how capital gets trapped.

### Full unit economics (the board-game example)
$12 buy → $34.99 sale is NOT $22.99 profit. After referral fee $5.25, FBA fee $5.92, storage $0.32:
net ≈ $11.50. Always subtract buy cost, referral, FBA fees, storage, prep, AND a return allowance —
the return-allowance line "gets skipped all the time and makes weak buys look profitable."

### Policy-risk framing for 2026
- Ask "is this specific product safe to sell under current rules", not "is arbitrage legal".
- Three predictable ways new sellers lose: buy first / check gating later; source from retailers whose
  receipts won't survive a complaint; chase headline ROI without checking price stability + seller count.
- Ungated listings fill up fast with sellers who found the same deal; restricted brands can carry the
  margin but approvals/documentation decide if it's usable; brand-sensitive listings tie up cash even
  with genuine inventory.
- Focus on **sellable margin, not spreadsheet margin**.

### Keepa read (what works / what fails)
Works: checking the chart before buying quantity — price stability, regular sales signs, livable seller
count, no pattern of Amazon jumping on the listing. Fails: treating the current Buy Box as a reliable
future selling price. BSR is a filter, not a decision.

### FBA & prep
Prefers FBA for standard products; cites The Selling Guys: partnered UPS rates save 30–50% on inbound
shipping, and Buy Box ≈ 70%+ of sales on a listing. Prep sloppiness is where cheap inventory becomes
expensive (condition complaints, stranded stock).

### FAQ anchors
- Common floor: **30% ROI and $5 net profit per unit** on a standard arbitrage buy.
- Suspension triggers: restricted categories/brands, inauthenticity complaints, brand IP complaints.
  Defense = boring systems: eligibility before purchase, clean receipts/invoices, avoid red-flag listings.
- Scaling: reinvest first, repricer before deeper sourcing software, delegate tasks not standards
  (write the buying framework down before hiring a VA).
