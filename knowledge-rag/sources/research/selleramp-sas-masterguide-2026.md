## Takeaways

- **Settings first.** Every SAS calculation depends on account settings (inbound shipping rate, prep cost). Enable Offer Summary and Buy Box Analysis panels manually; wrong settings silently mislead the Profit Calculator and Max Cost.
- **Shortcuts + panel reordering.** SAS lets you reorder panels in the Chrome extension and jump via shortcut buttons (Profit Calculator, Charts) instead of scrolling — meaningful time savings sourcing at volume.
- **Estimated Sales exclamation mark.** An "!" next to an estimated-sales number means the estimate is shared across all variations (not per-variation) — easy to misread demand if missed.
- **Alerts are a prompt to look closer, not an auto-skip.** E.g. a "suspected private label" flag means check whether the brand truly dominates the buy box or third parties still share it, before passing. Color coding: green = fine, red = serious, amber = investigate, blue = informational.
- **BSR is category-relative.** A "high" rank in a huge category (Clothing, Home & Kitchen) can still mean healthy sales; the same rank in a small category (Grocery) may not. Use the Ranks & Prices panel to gauge category size before judging a rank in isolation.
- **Max Cost can be overridden by judgment.** Max Cost is the ceiling price for a given profit rule, but a low-cost item that can't mathematically hit a flat $3 target may still be worth buying if it doubles your money — don't treat the default rule as absolute on cheap items.
- **Profit Calculator as a discount-math tool.** Use the cost field to subtract coupons, add sales tax, work out multi-buy/bulk per-unit cost, and apply percentage discounts — many OA deals are only profitable after stacking these, so recalculate before passing on a deal that looks break-even at retail.
- **Charts: watch offer-count direction vs price together.** Climbing competition + dropping price = warning sign; offer counts fluctuating due to sell-through/restock while Buy Box price stays stable = healthier market. Multi-year chart views reveal seasonal demand spikes (holidays) — useful for buying ahead of season.
- **Notes vs Tags.** Notes for product-specific reminders (where sourced, what discount worked); Tags for repeatable categories across ASINs ("Good for Q4", "Recheck when back in stock") — supports a persistent, revisitable lead bank.
- **Google Sheets export** for building a structured lead pipeline (out-of-stock opportunities, store-specific buys, team research) — relevant to the project's lead-tracking workflow.
- **Storefront stalking still the core beginner method** per this 2026 guide: open seller storefronts, check brands/categories carried, verify the numbers with SAS before treating a lead as real.
