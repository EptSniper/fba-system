# Amazon FBA Accounting & Bookkeeping 2026 (Eightx)

- **Source URL:** https://eightx.co/blog/amazon-fba-accounting-bookkeeping
- **Author:** Leandro D'Elia, Fractional CFO, Eightx
- **Published:** 2026-03-12 (modified 2026-06-27)
- **Fetched:** 2026-06-30
- **Classification:** [practitioner] — CFO firm guidance, not Amazon policy
- **Topic:** fba_oa_mastery (finances & bookkeeping)

## Distilled takeaways

1. **[practitioner]** Never book the **bi-weekly Amazon settlement deposit as "sales."** Decompose every
   settlement into its parts — gross sales, referral fee, FBA fulfillment fee, storage, advertising, refunds,
   reimbursements — and book each on an **accrual basis**. Recording the net deposit as revenue understates
   sales and hides expenses; the article's worked example: a $50,000 deposit actually represents ~$78,500 gross
   sales, ~$27,255 in fees, and ~$4,150 refunds. (eightx.co)
2. **[practitioner]** Keep **Amazon fees below gross margin as variable expenses, not in COGS.** Product cost
   is COGS; Amazon's 25–40% in fees is contribution-margin erosion. Burying fees in COGS makes gross margin
   look artificially low and muddies due diligence.
3. **[practitioner]** Use a **clearing account + deposits-in-transit asset account** and reconcile to the
   settlement report every period. Skipping this lets an unexplained balance drift (the article cites real
   drifts of $50K–$500K) that later forces a write-off.
4. **[practitioner]** Returns are a **net-zero COGS adjustment**: reverse the revenue and restore the unit to
   inventory; don't treat a refund as a new expense.
5. **[practitioner]** Maintain **separate channel P&Ls** (Amazon vs DTC vs retail) as analytical views — most
   accounting systems can't model Amazon as its own entity without double-counting, so slice by channel.
6. **Relevance to this project:** confirms the realized-ROI / outcome tracking (leads → outcomes) must subtract
   *all* fee components and refunds to compute true per-unit profit, not Seller Central's headline number —
   directly supports the honesty rule on `outcomes` data.
