# Amazon FBA Cash Flow Management (The FBA Guys)

Data-backed cash-flow discipline piece. Especially relevant to a small-budget OA operator where inventory
timing is the whole constraint.

## The core problem: inventory asks for cash before the sale
- Cash leaves for supplier deposit + balance + freight/duty/inspection/prep/placement **before** the customer
  buys; Amazon pays **after** the sale. That timing gap is the cash-flow problem, and **growth makes it
  worse** — more sales need bigger POs that arrive before the extra profit accumulates. "You can grow yourself
  into a cash crunch." The P&L records profit over a period; **cash has to survive the sequence.**

## Benchmark data (n≈8,503 FBA valuations)
- Average business carried **$150,204 inventory against $1,477,639 annual sales = ~17% of sales in stock**
  (~70% of estimated annual SDE). A business can look profitable while most owner benefit is tied up in stock.
- **Inventory-to-sales vs valuation:** <5% → $1.69M avg valuation; 5–10% → $1.41M; 10–20% → $1.27M; 20–35% →
  $816K; 35%+ → $338K. (Not a formula — lower-inventory businesses are also cleaner/faster — but buyers feel
  the same cash pressure operators do.)
- **Inventory-to-sales vs turn speed:** turning every few weeks → 10.6%; every few months → 14.7%; several
  months → 24.7%; **year-or-more → 75.8%.** Fast turns = more chances to correct (cash + reorder errors +
  demand shifts show up sooner).
- **Declining sales inflate the ratio even if units don't change** (the denominator drops): increased-a-lot
  14.3% → stable 18.1% → declined 22.2% → declined-a-lot 26.5%. Inventory "normal" in growth becomes heavy in
  decline.

## The cash conversion cycle
Supplier terms + production + freight + receiving + sell-through + Amazon payout + reserve/delay. Operator's
version of the question: **"if this reorder goes wrong, what else has to wait?"** (payroll, ads, loan, owner
draw, taxes). A markdown, holding price, borrowing to reorder, and cutting ad spend are **all cash-flow
decisions** — the mistake is treating them separately. Cutting ads can worsen it (rank sags while inventory
is heavy). Put the cash-flow review next to the operating review.

## Working capital sizing (planning recipe)
1) Estimate cash for the next full inventory cycle. 2) Add freight/duty/prep/placement. 3) Add ads + opex for
the period. 4) Add taxes/debt service/owner draws that hit before cash returns. 5) Subtract reliable incoming
cash. 6) Keep a timing-miss reserve. Better question than "can I afford this order?": **"what does this order
prevent me from doing before the cash comes back?"**

## Credit lines help but don't fix bad reorder habits
- With a line: avg $1.91M sales, 14.9% inv-to-sales; without: $1.00M, 19.2% (mostly because bigger, mature
  businesses qualify — causality is messy). Debt is a **tool when the cash cycle is repeatable and the line
  bridges timing**; **dangerous when it hides weak margin / stale demand / inventory that should've been
  marked down.** "The borrowed dollar needs a return date."

## Weekly cash-flow system (make timing visible)
Track: cash on hand; Amazon receivables + expected payout dates; inventory on hand/inbound/on order; open
supplier deposits + balances; ad spend (actual + planned); taxes/debt/payroll/owner draws; reorder points +
lead times by SKU; and a **13-week cash forecast** (where the separate spreadsheets finally talk to each
other). Tie it to SKU-level unit economics + settlement-report review so Amazon deposits aren't mistaken for
clean revenue. "Seller Central doesn't know what else your bank account has promised."

## OA takeaway
This is the discipline version of the ai-brain rules the project already holds (≤20% capital per ASIN,
3-month inventory rule, start 3–5 units): for a tiny bankroll, **inventory-to-sales and turn speed are the
cash-survival signals**, and every reorder should pass "what does this prevent?" before "can I afford it?"
