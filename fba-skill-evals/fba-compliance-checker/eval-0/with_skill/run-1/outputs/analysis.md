# Eligibility Check — Nike Air Force 1 (sneakers)

**Your question:** "Found Nike Air Force 1s at a Nike outlet sale, can flip on Amazon for double — should I grab them?"

I only answer the **"am I allowed to sell it?"** half here. The **"does it make money?"** half (ROI, fees, demand) is a separate question handled by the deal analyst — and I'll tell you below why that question doesn't even matter yet.

---

## ELIGIBILITY CHECK — Nike / Air Force 1

- **Gating:** **Hard-gated for almost every account.** Nike is one of the most commonly brand-gated names on Amazon. Nike pulled its official wholesale relationship with Amazon in 2019, and the brand is routinely restricted for third-party resellers. New/beginner accounts are extremely unlikely to be auto-ungated, and the ungating path (if one exists for you at all) typically demands **invoices from a Nike-authorized distributor** — which a retail/outlet receipt is **not**. An outlet receipt will not ungate you.

- **IP / brand risk: HIGH.** Nike is explicitly on the operation's **avoid-list** (IP-aggressive / commonly gated) in `references/oa-criteria.md`. Nike runs an active brand-protection and IP-enforcement program and is well known for filing complaints against resellers. Footwear is also one of the highest-counterfeit, highest-scrutiny categories on Amazon — even a genuine pair can trigger an authenticity complaint, and the burden of proof lands on you.

- **Hazmat / meltable / expiry / oversize:** None apparent. Sneakers aren't hazmat, meltable, or dated. (This is the *only* clean line — and it doesn't save the deal.)

- **Condition / variation notes:** Shoes are a **variation listing nightmare** — size/color children each have their own eligibility and their own offer dynamics. Even if you somehow cleared the brand gate, you could be eligible on some size/color children and not others. Footwear also carries a high return rate (fit), which erodes any "double your money" math fast.

- **Receipt problem (decisive):** Amazon's authorized-invoice standard for branded goods generally wants a **wholesale/distributor invoice**, not a consumer outlet receipt. So even if a complaint hits and you're asked to prove sourcing, an outlet sale receipt is likely to fail the documentation test.

---

## VERDICT: **BLOCKED** (do not buy for Amazon resale)

This is one of the clearest no-go combinations for a beginner: an **avoid-list brand**, a **hard-gated category**, a **high-counterfeit product type**, and a **non-qualifying receipt**. The "flip for double" upside is real-sounding but irrelevant — if you can't list it, the margin is zero, and if you list it wrong, you risk an IP complaint that can suspend the account. The account is the one asset you can't afford to lose, so caution wins here.

---

## Decisive next step

**List-before-you-buy.** Before spending a dollar, go into **Seller Central → Add a Product**, search the exact Air Force 1 ASIN, and try to create an offer:
- If you see **"You need approval to list this product"** / **"Apply to sell"** with a brand restriction → that's the hard gate. **Don't buy.**
- If (unlikely) you can list freely, still treat Nike footwear as high IP/counterfeit risk and confirm the approval path **before** buying — and know that an outlet receipt probably won't satisfy a future invoice request.

If you want to pursue branded sneakers seriously later, the only safe route is genuine ungating with **Nike-authorized distributor invoices**, not retail/outlet receipts.
