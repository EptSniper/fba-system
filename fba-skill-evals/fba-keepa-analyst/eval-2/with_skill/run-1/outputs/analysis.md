### 2. Offer count dropped from 40 → 2 → this is an IP-cliff fingerprint, not a "sellers sold through" story

This is the part where you need me to be skeptical *for* you, because a drop in offers can mean two very different things and they have opposite verdicts:

- **The good version (gentle decline):** offers grind down over weeks/months as sellers genuinely sell through their stock. Flat or declining offer count is normally a *good* sign — it means the listing isn't getting piled into and the price isn't about to tank.
- **The bad version (a cliff):** offer count **collapses fast and never recovers** — e.g. 56 → 1, or in your case **40 → 2**. That shape is the classic **IP cliff**: the brand owner filed intellectual-property complaints and Amazon swept the resellers off the listing in a short window. The skill calls this out explicitly and rates it **"worse than a price drop"** — because it's not a market signal, it's an enforcement signal. The sellers didn't *choose* to leave; they were *removed*.

A drop from 40 all the way down to **2** is steep enough that I'd treat it as an IP cliff until proven otherwise, **not** as healthy sell-through. Two things tell you which version you're looking at:

- **Did it recover?** If offers crashed to 2 and then climbed back up toward 40, it was probably a temporary stock/price event. If it crashed to 2 and **stayed low**, that's the cliff — and that's the worse outcome.
- **Who are the 2 left?** If one of the two remaining offers is **Amazon and/or the brand itself**, that confirms the enforcement read: the brand cleared the listing and now controls it. (Brand-as-seller on its own listing is itself a separate red-flag guard — they kick resellers off.)

Given that Amazon is also winning 60% of the Buy Box, the most likely story is that this listing is **brand- and/or Amazon-controlled**, and the 40→2 crash was resellers being removed or squeezed out — exactly the population you'd be trying to join.
